Outdoor Audience Measurement Design & Methodology

The MOVE AMS system is built on the back of the Zenith Transport Model which predicts movement of a population as they go about their day to day business. 

By itself this isn't sufficient information to adequately measure Out-of-Home (OOH) audience so significant enhancements have been made to Zenith to produce the Zenith for Outdoor system which combines a comprehensive travel and transport model in association with discrete environmental models to model audience interaction with OOH faces of a variety of formats.

The key enhancement provided by Zenith for Outdoor are:

  • Specialised models for special destinations such as airports, rail stations, bus terminals and shopping centres
  • Population synthesis to provide rich demographic information
  • Reach and Frequency modelling to analyse people's behaviour over time instead of on a single day
  • Visibility adjustment modelling to determine a person's Likelihood To See (LTS) rather than their Opportunity To See (OTS)

Airport model

The Airport Model was based upon a 12 month online survey which tracked the travel behaviour of 5,000 people living within a given market and visiting domestic and/or international airports.

Visitors were divided into travellers and non-travellers as they exhibited different travel behaviour based on their purpose of journey. Those meeting and greeting passengers, for instance, would not have access to advertising faces beyond the airport security gates in international terminals.

A number of attractors at airport terminals were used to determine the flow of audiences past these advertising faces. These included:

  • Check-in counters
  • Security screening
  • Retail areas
  • Baggage claim
  • Taxi ranks
  • Club lounges
  • Food courts
  • Airport entrances
  • Arrival/departure gates

Station Model

Transport advertising within railway stations and bus terminals can be viewed at the entrances to concourses, on the concourses themselves, and on and across platforms.

The allocation of audiences within a railway station is determined by the number of entrances to the station, the number of platforms in the station, and the number of platform entrances.

Outdoor advertising faces have been classified within the MOVE system to reflect their exact position within the railway or bus station against individually coded maps for each location.

Shopping Centre Model

Zenith takes people to the door of each shopping centre, allocating shopping, employment, personal business and recreational trips to individual shopping centres.

The Zenith for Outdoor system builds on top of this base to provide a separate model which can ascertain how these shopping centre visitors move around within the centre, and, importantly, how they interact with internal advertising faces.

Surveys of more than 6,000 shopping centre visitors were conducted at a number of locations to identify what particular attractors influenced their movements. Advertising faces were then allocated according to the following shopping centre and site classification process, which the survey identified as being important to the modelling of movements within a centre.

Reach and Frequency Model

The purpose of the R&F Model is to understand how the audience that passes a sign increases over time. Since the Zenith transport model accurately predicts the number of people travelling on a single day, the R&F model extends this to the maximum length of a campaign in the system. The Zenith Model is a traditional four-step transport model, and thus has four main behavioural components it attempts to capture:

  • How many trips will a household make on an average day, and for what purpose? (Trip Production)
  • Where will they travel to fulfil their trip purpose? (Trip Distribution)
  • By what mode will they choose to get there? (Mode Choice)
  • By what route, or by which service, will they choose to get there? (Route Choice)

Given that these are the four major ‘dimensions’ of the Zenith model, it is logical to look at how these behaviours vary over time. Thus, the four primary components of the R&F model correspond to each of these Zenith components.

Cascade Model (Trip Production Variability Model)

The ‘Cascade Model’ is the name given to the Trip Production Variability Model used within the MOVE AMS. It is designed as an extension to the Zenith Trip Production Model (ZTPM). The aim of the Cascade Model is to reliably estimate the number of trips an individual will make for a given trip purpose over an extended period of time, such as 84 days. Like the Zenith Trip Production Model (ZTPM), the Cascade Model uses demographic variables such as occupational status to predict the number of trips but unlike the ZTPM which works at a household level the Cascade model aims to model the trips made by individuals.

The key trip purposes to be considered in the Cascade Model are:

  • Home-Based-Work
  • Home-Based-Shopping
  • Home-Based-Education
  • Home-Based-Education
  • Home-Based-Other
  • Work-Based
  • Shopping-Based
  • Other-Based
  • To-Or-From-Airport

The ZTPM uses such household characteristics as the number of workers in the household. The Cascade Model, on the other hand, looks at individual characteristics, such as whether or not a person is a worker. Analysis of the data shows that whether a person is the Main Grocery Buyer (MGB) and their occupation are the most important factors in this decision. A person's occupation is broken down to one of following categories:

  • Full-time worker
  • Part-time worker
  • Unemployed
  • Secondary student
  • Tertiary student
  • Home duties
  • Retired

Swinger Model (Trip Distribution Variability Model)

The 'Swinger Model' is the name given to the Trip Distribution Variability Model used within the MOVE AMS. In the same way the Cascade Model extends Zenith’s Trip Production Model, the Swinger Model functions as an extension to the Zenith Trip Distribution Model (ZTDM).

Trip Distribution is the process of assigning destinations to trips, based on the cost of getting there (distance, time, public transport fare/car parking charges, etc.), and their relative attractiveness (such as number of employees for a home-to-work trip).For the destination variability, the key question identified was:

For a person who has made t trips to d different destinations for a given purpose, and is about to make another trip of that purpose, what is the likelihood that the trip will be to a new (unvisited) destination.

This likelihood can be expressed as P(d→d+1|t→t+1), which represents the probability P of the number of destinations, d, increasing, given that the number of trips is increasing from t to t+1. The primary aim of the Swinger Model then, is to:

Reliably estimate the number of destinations an individual will have visited for a given trip purpose after a given number of trips

Through analysis of survey data it was determined that this probability needed to be modelled as a function of both d and t. While the relation for this probability may differ depending on the trip maker’s occupation and whether or not it is a weekday or weekend, there was not sufficient data to show this with any confidence. However, a very strong correlation was found with the trip purpose.

The model will assume that each person has only one place of residence. This assumption simplifies the application of the model. Technically, for any to-home trip purposes, only one home zone can be assigned. In contrast, for other trip purposes at least some variability was found, which this model will capture. The purposes considered are:

  • Trips to place of work
  • Trips to place of shopping
  • Trips to place of recreation
  • Trips to place of education
  • Trips to other locations (not education, home, work, shopping or recreation)

For these purposes the probability of visiting a new destination is a function of both d and t, provided a trip of that purpose has been made. For each of the purposes with destination variability a function describing the probability of visiting a new destination needs to be obtained. These probabilities were found to correlate most closely with d/t, or the ratio of destinations to trips.

Mode Choice Variability Model (MCVM)

In Zenith, Mode Choice relates to the competing attractiveness of taking a car, public transport (PT), or walking/cycling, for any given trip. The Mode Choice Variability Model is designed to capture to what extent an individual repeats their choice of mode for different trips. Survey results showed that people do repeat their mode choice between Car and PT, however no such bias was observed for walking/cycling trips.

The MCVM then works by considering each individual to be randomly assigned a mode choice percentile, which indicates where they lie along the Car vs PT spectrum. For example, a person at the 0th percentile will choose PT for every trip, a person at the 100th percentile will always choose Car. At the 20th percentile, a person will choose Car 80-100% of the time for all purposes except Home Based Education and Other Based which have significantly more PT utilisation.

Route Choice Variability Model

Route choice variability is where, for a repeated trip (say, driving to the same destination for work on two consecutive days), different routes are chosen. Due to the limitations of the online-based survey method, however, no data about route choice could be collected, so no route variability could be included. Note that none of the GHTS datasets include any route information either.

From some anecdotal evidence from an A-GPS pilot study performed in Madrid by Cuende Infometrics, however, there appears to be a very limited amount of route choice variability, and so we have adopted the assumption that, for a repeated trip, the same route is always chosen.

Formats

The MOVE AMS is a world leader in the variety of formats that it provides coverage for. The formats currently supported by MOVE are: 

Roadside

  • Billboards over 25 square metres, including supersites and spectaculars.
  • Billboards 25 square metres and under, including posters.
  • Bus and tram shelters.
  • Bus and tram externals
  • Kiosks and free standing panels.

Transport

  • Railway/bus stations.
  • Airports.
  • Bus and tram internals.

Retail / Lifestyle

  • Shopping Centres

Importantly, due to it's modular nature, the MOVE AMS system is open to extension and research on incorporating additional formats into the system is planned in the future.
